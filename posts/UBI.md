# A case for Universal Basic Income

## Introduction

For me Universal Basic Income (UBI) seemed like a bit of a pipe dream; it seemed like it would be too expensive, too difficult to implement. My view started to change when I did a bit of research and had a look into what implementation of UBI would look like.

## What is UBI?

UBI is an alternative way to provide support to the less fortunate in society. Currently the system we have in place takes the form of Universal Credit and Pension Credit, or as most people know it: benefits. Universal Credit (UC) is dished out to those who are "on low income, out of work or cannot work" according to the Government website. This means the Government picks and chooses who needs and who deserves benefits to support their living costs, this absent-by-default system means lots of time and money is put into sorting out who UC should be given to.

UBI on the other hand works as a present-by-default system and those who don't need the supplementary income will pay it back in tax. This method essentially cuts out the middle man and could save money in the long run!

## Let's look a bit closer at the disadvantages of an absent-by-default system

So, what is the government actually spending money on when picking and choosing who UC goes to? The cost of the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) delivering its services in 2025/26 was £10.6 billion, this is essentially the fund that goes towards paying the staff and other administrative costs that the DWP needs in order to run. This may not seem like a lot in comparison to the £309.5 billion that the DWP spends in total, but we shall address this later.

Paying the staff to distribute UC is one thing but the actual distributing (and doing it correctly!) is very difficult. Working out the need of each individual person, including things like how sick someone is, is almost impossible to get right and the attempt to do so means that the Government has to get very involved in peoples private lives, something that lots of people will not be comfortable with and will often be humiliated by.

Even if we ignore the issues mentioned up to this point and assume everything is working as hoped, the system still doesn't provide security. The eligibility status of an individual can change at any point and often people don't know if their application will be approved, or even if it will be approved at all! This lack of certainty makes people's lives stressful and more difficult. The time that applications take to come back can also result in other issues, for example: if someone’s circumstances change by the time they start to receive payments this means more administrative costs are required, especially if someone’s situation worsened to the point of homelessness.

Let's now talk about the bit that lots of more fiscally conservative folk may find interesting: benefits can cause people to not want to work. This is something I would hear my family talking about and write off as an irrelevant point but, after reading ACT Now by Common Sense Policy Group (which was the main source inspiring this article) I changed my mind. ACT Now states "If you have to demonstrate unemployment in order to continue to qualify, then you have an incentive _not_ to take some part-time work.". It also goes on to talk about other perverse incentives such as the "incentive to remain sick". Essentially, while the majority of people claiming UC do want to work, the steps to get to the point of having solid, secure work mean risking the security net that people, especially those with children, are too scared to lose out on.

## So how does UBI fix this?

It feels quite obvious how a present-by-default system fixes a lot of these issues. We have already touched on the point of reducing on administrative costs by not having to choose who gets UBI; everyone receives the same amount. It is also obvious to see that UBI makes it a lot easier for claimants given they don't have to go through any procedures at all to begin receiving.

Providing security is something UBI really succeeds at, there is never any worry about what you are going to receive and when, you will always have a consistent, minimum stream of income no matter what. This system would reduce on stress, improve the welfare significantly of those struggling the most. Circumstances changing is now a non-issue and perverse incentives are removed as working will always provide more income and there are no risks of the security net disappearing.

## What would UBI look like in practice?

I will be drawing strongly from Common Sense Policy Group's (CSPG) ACT Now for this and using their idea of what UBI in practice could look like.

CSPG recommends that the government "introduce a basic income for all permanent UK residents, initially set at £75 per week for all over 18s, with £50 for each child.". They also recommend reducing the personal allowance (the initial amount of income that is untaxed) from £12,570 to £750 (saving about £86 billion) and increases in taxes by 3p on the pound for each tax bracket (to 23p basic, 43p higher, 48p additional) in order to fund this scheme's £274 billion cost (also helped by the £10 billion saved earlier in admin costs).

This starter scheme immediately benefits the vast majority of the population, increasing the income of the bottom ten percent by almost 150% and only reducing the top decile by less than 10% with the rest of the population increasing or staying around the same. The short-term effect on poverty is effective reducing the number of adults in poverty by 23% and the number of children by 54%. Those in the middle who wouldn't have received welfare assistance previously also benefit from small to significant increases in income but also by the security it brings allowing people to feel safer in things such as changing careers, getting more training or even returning to education and pursuing their dreams.

## Conclusion

Universal Basic Income really does seem like an incredible replacement for our current (arguably failing) benefits system. It's not a pipe dream, it's not something that can only exist in some sort of socialist utopia, it's a real policy that I truly believe more people should pay attention to. I also recommend that people read ACT Now, it's really interesting and looks into UBI more deeply but also a whole lot of other policy ideas that are very interesting and work alongside UBI for a better future for the UK. 

Schemes like this that benefit the working people of our country the most are often overlooked and prevented from getting through parliament by perverse, money-backed powers who care not for the working people of this nation. If policies like this sound like something you would be interested in, I recommend looking into local pressure groups and political organisations that have your best interests in mind and seeing what you can do to help. We can change this country for the better.
