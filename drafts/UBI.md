# A case for Universal Basic Income

## Introduction

For me Universal Basic Income (UBI) seemed like a bit of a pipe dream; it seemed like it would be too expensive, too difficult to implement. My view started to change when I did a bit of research and had a look into what implementation of UBI would look like.

## What is UBI?

UBI is an alternative way to provide support to the less fortunate in society. Currently the system we have in place takes the form of Universal Credit and Pension Credit, or as most people know it: benefits. Universal Credit (UC) is dished out to those who are "on low income, out of work or cannot work" according to the Government website. This means the Government picks and chooses who needs and who deserves benefits to support their living costs, this absent-by-default system means lots of time and money is put into sorting out who UC should be given to.

UBI on the other hand works as a present-by-default system and those who don't need the supplementary income will pay it back in tax. This method essentially cuts out the middle man and could save money in the longrun!

## Let's look a bit closer at the disadvantages of an absent-by-default system

So what is the government actually spending money on when picking and choosing who UC goes to? The cost of the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) delivering it's services in 2025/26 was £10.6 billion, this is essentially the fund that goes towards paying the staff and other administrative costs that the DWP needs in order to run. This may not seem like a lot in comparison to the £309.5 billion that the DWP spends in total but we shall address this later.

Paying the staff to distribute UC is one thing but the actual distributing (and doing it correctly!) is very difficult. Working out the need of each individual person, including things like how sick someone is, is almost impossible to get right and the attempt to do so means that the Government has to get very involved in peoples private lives, something that lot's of people will not be comfortable with and will often be humiliated by.

Even if we ignore the issues mentioned up to this point and assume everything is working as hoped, the system still doesn't provide security. The elegibility status of an individual can change at any point and often people don't know if their application will be approved, or even if it will be approved at all! This lack of certainty makes people's lives stressful and more difficult. The time that applications take to come back can also result in other issues, for example: if someones circumstances change by the time they start to receive payments this means more administrative costs are required, especially if someones situation worsened to the point of homelessness.

Let's now talk about the bit that lots of more fiscally conservative folk may find interesting: benefits can cause people to not want to work. This is something I would hear my family talking about and write off as an irrelevant point but, after reading Act Now by the Common Sense Policy Group (which was the main source inspiring this article) I changed my mind. Act Now states "If you have to demonstrate unemployment in order to continue to qualify, then you have an incentive _not_ to take some part-time work.". It also goes on to talk about other perverse incentives such as the "incentive to remain sick". Incentives like this are not helpful for the aim of a productive society.
